# Complete Physics Materials Catalog: Oscillation Properties and Cymatic Patterns

## Part 1: Fundamental States of Matter

### 1.1 Solids - Constrained Oscillation

**General principle:**
Atoms locked in fixed positions, but continuously vibrate around equilibrium sites.

---

#### **Crystalline Solids**

**Lattice structure:** Atoms arranged in periodic 3D pattern.

**Oscillation modes:**

**Acoustic phonons (low frequency):**
- Entire lattice regions oscillate together
- Like sound waves through material
- Frequency: 10¹⁰ - 10¹³ Hz (GHz to THz range)
- Wavelength: Many atomic spacings

**Optical phonons (high frequency):**
- Adjacent atoms oscillate in opposite directions
- Like internal vibrations within unit cell
- Frequency: 10¹³ - 10¹⁴ Hz (infrared range)
- Wavelength: Few atomic spacings

---

**Temperature dependence:**

```
T near 0 K (absolute zero):
- Minimal oscillation amplitude
- Only quantum zero-point motion
- Atoms barely wiggle
- Material very rigid

T increases:
- Oscillation amplitude increases
- Atoms vibrate more vigorously
- Material expands (thermal expansion)
- Elastic modulus decreases (softer)

T approaches melting point:
- Oscillations become violent
- Atoms occasionally escape wells
- Lattice defects increase
- Material softens dramatically

T = melting point:
- Oscillation amplitude exceeds lattice constraint
- Atoms escape fixed positions
- Lattice breaks down
- Solid → Liquid transition
```

---

**Specific examples:**

**Diamond (Carbon, sp³ bonded):**
- Very high frequency oscillation (strong bonds)
- Low amplitude (stiff lattice)
- Phonon frequency: ~40 THz
- Debye temperature: 2230 K (high)
- Result: Hardest natural material, high melting point

**Lead (Pb):**
- Low frequency oscillation (weak bonds)
- Large amplitude (soft lattice)
- Phonon frequency: ~3 THz
- Debye temperature: 105 K (low)
- Result: Soft, malleable, low melting point (327°C)

**Quartz (SiO₂ crystal):**
- Anisotropic oscillation (different in different directions)
- Piezoelectric (oscillation creates voltage)
- Natural resonance frequency: ~32.768 kHz (used in watches)
- Very stable frequency (temperature-independent)

---

#### **Amorphous Solids (Glasses)**

**Structure:** No long-range order, random atomic arrangement.

**Oscillation characteristics:**

**Boson peak (extra modes):**
- Excess vibrational modes at ~1 THz
- Not present in crystals
- Due to structural disorder
- Creates anomalous properties

**Anharmonicity:**
- Oscillations not pure sine waves
- Asymmetric potential wells
- Frequency depends on amplitude

**Relaxation dynamics:**
- Slow structural rearrangements
- Atoms gradually shift positions over time
- Not quite equilibrium (unlike crystals)

---

**Example: Silica glass (SiO₂ amorphous):**

```
Short timescale (<10⁻¹² s):
- Atoms oscillate in local potential wells
- Similar to crystal behavior
- High frequency vibrations

Medium timescale (10⁻⁹ - 10⁻³ s):
- Structural relaxations
- Atoms hop between configurations
- Beta-relaxation

Long timescale (>seconds):
- Viscous flow (very slow)
- Glass "flows" downward over centuries
- Alpha-relaxation (glass transition)
```

### 1.2 Liquids - Free but Coupled Oscillation

**General principle:**
Atoms not fixed in position, but still influenced by neighbors.

---

**Oscillation modes:**

**Individual atomic vibration:**
- Frequency: ~10¹² - 10¹³ Hz (THz)
- Each atom oscillates in "cage" of neighbors
- Cage lifetime: ~10⁻¹² seconds

**Collective density waves:**
- Sound waves propagate
- Frequency: 10⁶ - 10⁹ Hz (MHz - GHz for ultrasound)
- Wavelength: Micrometers to millimeters

**Capillary waves (surface):**
- Surface tension driven
- Frequency: 10² - 10⁴ Hz
- Wavelength: Millimeters
- Pattern: Ripples on water surface

---

**Temperature dependence:**

```
T near freezing:
- High viscosity
- Atoms move slowly between cages
- Long cage lifetime
- Oscillation highly damped
- More "solid-like"

T increases:
- Viscosity decreases
- Atoms escape cages quickly
- Short cage lifetime
- Less damping
- More "gas-like"

T near boiling:
- Very low viscosity
- Cage breaks down rapidly
- Almost free motion
- Approaching gas behavior
```

---

**Specific examples:**

**Water (H₂O):**

```
Molecular vibrations:
- O-H stretch: 110 THz (infrared absorption)
- H-O-H bend: 48 THz
- Librational motion (rotation): 20 THz

Hydrogen bond network oscillations:
- Collective H-bond stretching: 5-10 THz
- Network reorganization: 0.1-1 THz
- This creates water's anomalous properties

Sound speed: 1480 m/s (high for liquid)
Acoustic frequency range: MHz to GHz
```

**Liquid metals (e.g., Mercury):**

```
No molecular vibrations (atoms, not molecules)
Only collective modes:

Density oscillations: 1-10 THz
Surface oscillations: 100-1000 Hz
Acoustic modes: MHz-GHz

High electrical conductivity:
- Free electrons oscillate
- Plasma frequency: ~10¹⁶ Hz (UV range)
- Reflects light (shiny appearance)
```

**Liquid crystals:**

```
Orientational order (molecules aligned)
But no positional order (free to move)

Director oscillations:
- Collective molecular reorientation
- Frequency: 1-100 Hz
- Slow (viscous damping)

Used in displays:
- Apply voltage → molecules rotate
- Rotation is oscillatory (with AC voltage)
- Controls light transmission
```

### 1.3 Gases - Nearly Free Oscillation

**General principle:**
Molecules move freely, interact only during collisions.

---

**Oscillation modes:**

**Molecular vibrations (intramolecular):**

For diatomic molecules (e.g., N₂, O₂):
```
Bond stretch:
- Frequency: ~70 THz (infrared)
- Quantum: ν = (1/2π)√(k/μ)
  where k = bond stiffness, μ = reduced mass
```

For polyatomic molecules (e.g., CO₂, H₂O):
```
Multiple vibrational modes:
- Symmetric stretch
- Asymmetric stretch
- Bending modes
- Each with characteristic frequency
```

---

**Molecular rotations:**

```
Rotation about center of mass
Frequency depends on moment of inertia

Small molecules (H₂): ~10¹² - 10¹³ Hz (THz, far-infrared)
Large molecules: 10⁹ - 10¹¹ Hz (GHz, microwave)

Quantized: E = J(J+1)ℏ²/(2I)
where J = rotational quantum number, I = moment of inertia
```

---

**Translational motion:**

```
Not oscillatory (free motion between collisions)

But collective effects create oscillations:

Sound waves (pressure oscillations):
- Frequency: 20 Hz - 20 kHz (audible)
- Higher: Ultrasound (up to GHz)
- Wavelength: Millimeters to meters

Shock waves (nonlinear):
- Steep pressure front
- Contains high harmonics
- Frequency spectrum: Very broad
```

---

**Temperature dependence:**

```
T increases:
- Vibrational amplitude increases
- Rotational energy increases
- Translational speed increases

Room temperature (300 K):
- N₂ molecule average speed: 500 m/s
- Collision frequency: ~10¹⁰ per second
- Mean free path: ~100 nm
```

---

**Specific examples:**

**Air (78% N₂, 21% O₂, 1% Ar):**

```
N₂ vibration: 70 THz (infrared)
O₂ vibration: 47 THz (infrared)

Sound speed: 343 m/s (at 20°C)
Acoustic impedance: Low (easy to compress)

Thermal diffusion: Fast
Viscosity: Low
```

**Noble gases (He, Ne, Ar, etc.):**

```
Monoatomic (no vibration or rotation)
Only translational motion

But can have collective oscillations:
- Sound waves: Yes
- Plasma oscillations (if ionized): ~GHz-THz

Helium (He):
- Lightest, fastest molecules
- Average speed at 300K: 1400 m/s
- Very high thermal conductivity
- Sound speed: 1020 m/s (very high)
```

**Water vapor (steam):**

```
H₂O molecular vibrations:
- Symmetric stretch: 110 THz
- Asymmetric stretch: 112 THz
- Bending: 48 THz

Rotational spectrum:
- GHz to THz range
- Microwave ovens use: 2.45 GHz (rotational resonance)

Absorption:
- Strong in infrared (greenhouse gas)
- Microwave absorption (heating)
```

### 1.4 Plasma - Collective Oscillation Dominated

**General principle:**
Ionized gas, charged particles create collective electromagnetic oscillations.

---

**Oscillation modes:**

**Plasma oscillations (Langmuir waves):**
```
Electrons oscillate collectively
Ions remain nearly stationary (heavy)

Plasma frequency:
ωₚ = √(ne²/(ε₀mₑ))

Where:
- n = electron density
- e = electron charge
- mₑ = electron mass

Typical: 10⁹ - 10¹⁶ Hz (GHz to UV)

High density (metals): ~10¹⁶ Hz (optical)
Low density (ionosphere): ~10⁶ Hz (radio)
```

**Ion acoustic waves:**
```
Ions oscillate, electrons provide screening

Frequency: Much lower than plasma frequency
Typical: kHz to MHz

Speed: ~√(kT/M) where M = ion mass
```

**Alfvén waves:**
```
Magnetic field coupled to plasma
Like waves on magnetic field lines

Frequency: Hz to kHz
Speed: Alfvén velocity = B/√(μ₀ρ)
  where B = magnetic field, ρ = mass density

Important in:
- Solar corona
- Magnetosphere
- Fusion reactors
```

---

**Specific examples:**

**Lightning (atmospheric plasma channel):**

```
Extremely hot (~30,000 K)
Highly ionized air

Oscillations:
- Plasma frequency: ~THz
- Channel expansion: Shock wave at MHz-GHz
- RF emissions: kHz to GHz (broad spectrum)

Thunder:
- Acoustic shock from rapid expansion
- Frequency: 20 Hz - 20 kHz
```

**Fluorescent lamp plasma:**

```
Low pressure Hg vapor plasma

Electron oscillations: THz (UV emission)
Mercury line emission: Specific frequencies
- 254 nm (UV, most intense)
- 365 nm (UV)
- 436 nm (blue)
- 546 nm (green)

Phosphor oscillations:
- UV absorbed → electrons excited
- Relaxation → visible light emitted
- Decay time: ns to μs (phosphorescence)
```

**Solar corona:**

```
Very hot (1-3 million K)
Fully ionized hydrogen

Plasma frequency: ~GHz (high density at base)

Wave modes:
- Alfvén waves: Heats corona
- Ion acoustic waves: Propagate disturbances
- Magnetoacoustic waves: Combine magnetic + acoustic

Radio emissions: MHz to GHz (solar radio bursts)
```

**Tokamak fusion plasma:**

```
Magnetically confined
Temperature: 100-200 million K

Plasma frequency: ~100 GHz

Instabilities (oscillatory):
- Edge-localized modes (ELMs): kHz
- Sawtooth oscillations: 10-100 Hz
- Fishbone oscillations: ~10 kHz

These limit confinement (challenge for fusion)
```

## Part 2: Electromagnetic Properties and Oscillations

### 2.1 Dielectric Materials

**General principle:**
Electric field induces oscillating dipole moments.

---

**Polarization mechanisms:**

**Electronic polarization:**
```
Electron cloud oscillates relative to nucleus

Frequency: ~10¹⁵ - 10¹⁶ Hz (UV range)
Response time: ~10⁻¹⁶ seconds (very fast)

All materials have this
Determines optical properties (refractive index)
```

**Ionic polarization:**
```
Positive and negative ions oscillate relative to each other

Frequency: ~10¹² - 10¹⁴ Hz (infrared)
Response time: ~10⁻¹³ seconds

Only in ionic crystals (NaCl, ceramics)
Creates infrared absorption
```

**Orientational polarization:**
```
Permanent dipoles (molecules) rotate to align with field

Frequency: 10⁶ - 10¹⁰ Hz (radio to microwave)
Response time: ~10⁻⁹ seconds (slow)

Requires molecules to rotate
Strong in polar liquids (water)
Microwave heating uses this (2.45 GHz for water)
```

**Interfacial polarization:**
```
Charge accumulates at interfaces/grain boundaries

Frequency: 10⁻² - 10⁶ Hz (very low)
Response time: milliseconds to seconds

Only in heterogeneous materials
Important in ceramics, composites
```

---

**Dielectric loss (energy dissipation):**

When driving frequency matches polarization mechanism:
```
Maximum absorption occurs
Energy converted to heat
Material heats up

This is resonance absorption
Each mechanism has resonance band
```

---

**Specific examples:**

**Polystyrene (nonpolar polymer):**

```
Only electronic polarization
Dielectric constant: 2.5 (frequency-independent, optical to DC)
Low loss at all frequencies
Good insulator for electronics
```

**Barium titanate (BaTiO₃, ferroelectric ceramic):**

```
Complex polarization:
- Electronic: UV range
- Ionic: Infrared (~20 THz)
- Domain wall motion: kHz-MHz
- Interfacial: Hz-kHz

Dielectric constant: 1000-10,000 (very high)
Strong frequency dependence
High loss in domain wall regime

Used in: Capacitors, transducers, memory
```

**Water (H₂O, polar liquid):**

```
Strong orientational polarization
Relaxation frequency: ~20 GHz

Below 20 GHz:
- High dielectric constant (~80)
- Dipoles can follow field

Above 20 GHz:
- Decreasing dielectric constant
- Dipoles too slow

At 2.45 GHz:
- Strong absorption (microwave oven frequency)
- Dipoles lag field → energy dissipation
```

---

### 2.2 Magnetic Materials

**General principle:**
Magnetic moments (spins) oscillate and precess.

---

**Magnetic oscillation modes:**

**Larmor precession:**
```
Magnetic moment precesses around applied field
Like spinning top precessing in gravity

Precession frequency (Larmor frequency):
ω = γB

Where:
- γ = gyromagnetic ratio (particle-specific)
- B = magnetic field strength

For electron: γ/2π = 28 GHz/Tesla
For proton: γ/2π = 42.6 MHz/Tesla (used in MRI)

Frequency: MHz to GHz depending on field
```

**Ferromagnetic resonance (FMR):**
```
All magnetic moments precess together (coherent)

Resonance frequency:
ω = γ√(B(B + μ₀M))

Where M = magnetization

Typical: 1-100 GHz for ferromagnets
Strong absorption at resonance
Used in: Microwave devices, magnetic recording
```

**Spin waves (magnons):**
```
Propagating oscillations of spin direction
Like sound waves in magnetic system

Frequency: GHz to THz
Wavelength: nm to μm

Dispersion relation: ω ~ k² (quadratic, not linear like phonons)

Low energy excitations
Important in magnetic materials
```

**Domain wall oscillations:**
```
Magnetic domain boundaries oscillate

Frequency: kHz to MHz
Large amplitude possible
Causes energy loss (hysteresis)

Limits high-frequency performance of transformers
```

---

**Temperature effects:**

```
T near 0 K:
- All spins aligned (quantum ground state)
- Only magnons (quantized excitations)
- Low damping

T increases:
- More magnons excited
- Spin alignment decreases
- Magnetization decreases

T = Curie temperature (Tc):
- Spontaneous magnetization vanishes
- Ferromagnet → Paramagnet transition
- Critical fluctuations (all frequencies present)

T > Tc:
- Only paramagnetic (individual spin precession)
- No collective modes
- Rapid thermal fluctuations: THz
```

---

**Specific examples:**

**Iron (Fe, ferromagnet):**

```
Curie temperature: 1043 K
Saturation magnetization: 2.2 Tesla

Ferromagnetic resonance: ~10-30 GHz (typical lab fields)
Spin wave frequencies: 1-100 GHz
Domain wall oscillation: 100 kHz - 1 MHz

Damping: Moderate
Response speed: ~ns (fast)

Used in: Transformers, motors, magnetic recording
```

**Ferrites (e.g., Fe₃O₄, ceramic ferromagnets):**

```
Lower saturation magnetization than metals
But electrically insulating (key advantage)

FMR frequency: 1-10 GHz
High resistance → Low eddy currents
Low loss at GHz frequencies

Used in: Microwave devices, antennas, radar
```

**Neodymium magnets (Nd₂Fe₁₄B, hard ferromagnet):**

```
Very high coercivity (hard to demagnetize)
Strong permanent magnets

Curie temperature: 585 K
Pinned domain walls (don't oscillate easily)

Not used for high-frequency (too slow to respond)
Used for: Static fields (motors, speakers, MRI)
```

**Antiferromagnets (e.g., NiO):**

```
Adjacent spins point opposite directions
No net magnetization

But internal oscillations:
- Antiferromagnetic resonance (AFMR): 100-1000 GHz (THz)
- Much higher than FMR
- Two spin sublattices oscillate

Very fast dynamics
Emerging applications: THz technology
```

### 2.3 Conducting Materials

**General principle:**
Free electrons oscillate in response to electric field.

---

**Electron oscillations:**

**Plasma oscillation (bulk):**
```
Collective electron density wave

Plasma frequency:
ωₚ = √(ne²/(ε₀mₑ))

For metals:
- n ~ 10²⁸ electrons/m³
- ωₚ ~ 10¹⁶ Hz (UV to soft X-ray)

Below ωₚ: Material reflects (opaque)
Above ωₚ: Material transmits (transparent)

Why metals are shiny: Reflect all visible light (below ωₚ)
```

**Drude response (AC conductivity):**
```
Electrons accelerated by field
Damped by collisions

Relaxation time (τ):
- Good conductors: ~10⁻¹⁴ seconds
- Frequency: ~10¹⁴ Hz

At low frequency (f << 1/τ):
- Electrons follow field
- High conductivity
- Phase shift: 90° (reactive)

At high frequency (f >> 1/τ):
- Electrons can't keep up
- Low conductivity
- Becomes insulator-like
```

---

**Skin effect:**

```
AC current confined to surface
Penetration depth:
δ = √(2/(ωμσ))

Where:
- ω = frequency
- μ = permeability
- σ = conductivity

At 60 Hz (power lines):
- Copper: δ ~ 8 mm

At 1 GHz (microwave):
- Copper: δ ~ 2 μm

High frequency → Current only at surface → Higher resistance
```

---

**Specific examples:**

**Copper (Cu, excellent conductor):**

```
Free electron density: 8.5 × 10²⁸ /m³
Plasma frequency: 3 × 10¹⁶ Hz (UV, ~100 eV)

DC conductivity: 5.96 × 10⁷ S/m (very high)
Relaxation time: 3 × 10⁻¹⁴ s

Optical properties:
- Visible light: Opaque, reflective (below ωₚ)
- UV: Begins to transmit (near ωₚ)
- X-ray: Transparent (above ωₚ)

Used in: Wiring, RF electronics (low loss)
```

**Graphene (2D carbon sheet):**

```
Extremely high mobility (~10⁶ cm²/Vs)
Long relaxation time: ~10⁻¹² s

Plasma frequency: Tunable (depends on carrier density)
- Can be in THz range
- Electrically adjustable

Optical response:
- 2.3% absorption per layer (constant across visible)
- Unique: Frequency-independent absorption

Emerging applications: Terahertz devices, transparent electrodes
```

**Superconductors (below Tc):**

```
Zero DC resistance
Infinite conductivity (in principle)

But AC response different:

London penetration depth (λL):
- Magnetic field penetrates to depth λL
- Typical: 50-500 nm
- Oscillates at applied frequency

Josephson oscillations:
- Two superconductors with thin barrier
- Apply DC voltage V → AC current at frequency f = 2eV/h
- Frequency: GHz range
- Used in: Voltage standards, SQUIDs, quantum computing
```

## Part 3: Optical and Photonic Materials

### 3.1 Transparent Dielectrics (Glass, Crystals)

**General principle:**
Light propagates with minimal absorption, but oscillates charged particles.

---

**Refractive index:**

```
Light speed in material: v = c/n
Where n = refractive index

n determined by electronic polarizability:
n² ≈ 1 + χ_electronic

Higher polarizability → Higher n

Examples:
- Vacuum: n = 1 (by definition)
- Air: n = 1.0003
- Water: n = 1.33
- Glass: n = 1.5-1.9
- Diamond: n = 2.42
```

---

**Dispersion (frequency dependence):**

```
n increases at higher frequency (usually)

Near absorption resonances:
- n varies rapidly
- Strong dispersion

Sellmeier equation (empirical):
n²(λ) = 1 + Σ[Bᵢλ²/(λ² - Cᵢ)]

Where Bᵢ, Cᵢ are material constants

Result:
- Blue light slower than red (higher n)
- Prism separates colors
- Rainbows
```

---

**Nonlinear optics:**

```
High intensity light → Electrons oscillate nonlinearly
Creates new frequencies

Second harmonic generation (SHG):
- Input: ω → Output: 2ω
- Frequency doubling
- Requires non-centrosymmetric crystal

Third harmonic generation (THG):
- Input: ω → Output: 3ω
- Works in any material (but weak)

Four-wave mixing (FWM):
- Inputs: ω₁, ω₂, ω₃ → Output: ω₄ = ω₁ + ω₂ - ω₃
- Used in: Parametric amplifiers, frequency combs
```

---

**Specific examples:**

**Fused silica (SiO₂ glass):**

```
Electronic resonances: Deep UV (~150 nm)
Infrared absorption: ~4 μm (Si-O stretching)

Transparent window: 200 nm - 2 μm
Includes all visible light

Refractive index: 1.46 (at 589 nm)
Dispersion: Low (good for lenses)

Nonlinear coefficient: Low (needs high intensity for nonlinear effects)

Used in: Optical fibers, lenses, windows
```

**Lithium niobate (LiNbO₃, nonlinear crystal):**

```
Non-centrosymmetric (allows SHG)
Very high nonlinear coefficient

Used for:
- Frequency doubling (e.g., 1064 nm → 532 nm, green laser pointer)
- Electro-optic modulators (fast optical switches)
- Waveguides (photonic circuits)

Refractive index: ~2.2 (high)
Birefringent (n depends on polarization)
```

**Diamond (C, sp³):**

```
Highest refractive index of any transparent material: n = 2.42

Wide transparency: UV to far-infrared
(No absorption in visible → colorless)

Dispersion: Very high
- Strong color separation in prisms
- "Fire" in cut diamonds

Raman oscillations:
- 1332 cm⁻¹ (40 THz)
- Strong, sharp peak
- Used for: Temperature sensing, Raman spectroscopy
```

### 3.2 Semiconductors

**General principle:**
Band gap determines optical properties, electrons oscillate across gap.

---

**Band gap absorption:**

```
Photon energy > Band gap → Absorbed (electron excited to conduction band)
Photon energy < Band gap → Transmitted (transparent)

Band gap energies:
- Silicon (Si): 1.1 eV (1100 nm, near-infrared)
- Gallium arsenide (GaAs): 1.43 eV (870 nm, infrared)
- Gallium nitride (GaN): 3.4 eV (365 nm, UV)

Visible light range: 1.7 - 3.1 eV (400-700 nm)

Silicon: Transparent to infrared, opaque to visible
GaN: Transparent to visible, opaque to UV
```

---

**Light emission (electroluminescence):**

```
Electron-hole recombination → Photon emitted
Photon energy ≈ Band gap energy

LEDs (Light Emitting Diodes):
- Forward bias → Electrons + holes injected
- Recombination at p-n junction
- Light emission

Laser diodes:
- High carrier density
- Stimulated emission (coherent)
- Optical cavity (mirrors)
- Laser output
```

---

**Carrier oscillations:**

```
Free electrons and holes oscillate in applied field

Plasma frequency (carrier plasma):
ωₚ = √(ne²/(ε₀ε_rm*))

Where:
- n = carrier density
- m* = effective mass

Typically: THz range for doped semiconductors

Can be tuned by:
- Doping (change n)
- Optical excitation (create carriers)
- Electrical bias
```

---

**Specific examples:**

**Silicon (Si):**

```
Band gap: 1.1 eV (indirect)

Indirect gap:
- Phonon required for electron transition
- Weak absorption/emission
- Not efficient for LEDs

But excellent for:
- Solar cells (absorbs infrared + visible)
- Photodetectors
- Integrated circuits

Plasma frequency (doped):
- n ~ 10²⁰ /cm³ → ωₚ ~ 10 THz
- Reflective in far-infrared
```

**Gallium arsenide (GaAs):**

```
Band gap: 1.43 eV (direct)

Direct gap:
- No phonon required
- Strong absorption/emission
- Efficient LEDs and lasers

Used in:
- Infrared LEDs (870 nm)
- Laser diodes (telecom)
- High-frequency electronics (5G, satellite)

Higher electron mobility than Si:
- Faster transistors
- But more expensive
```

**Gallium nitride (GaN):**

```
Band gap: 3.4 eV (wide, direct)

Emission: UV to blue (depending on composition)

Used in:
- Blue/white LEDs (revolutionized lighting)
- Blue laser diodes (Blu-ray)
- UV sterilization LEDs

Can be alloyed:
- InGaN: Blue-green (by adding In)
- AlGaN: UV (by adding Al)
- Tunable emission across visible
```

**Quantum dots (CdSe, PbS, etc.):**

```
Nanoscale semiconductors (2-10 nm diameter)

Quantum confinement:
- Smaller size → Larger effective band gap
- Tunable emission by size

CdSe quantum dots:
- 2 nm: Blue emission
- 6 nm: Red emission

Very narrow emission spectrum (~30 nm width)
High quantum efficiency

Used in:
- Display technology (QLED TVs)
- Biological imaging
- Solar cells (multi-junction)
```

### 3.3 Metamaterials

**General principle:**
Artificial structures with electromagnetic properties not found in nature.

---

**Negative index materials:**

```
Both permittivity (ε) and permeability (μ) negative

Refractive index: n = -√(εμ)

Consequences:
- Negative refraction (bends opposite direction)
- Reversed Doppler effect
- Reversed Cherenkov radiation

Implementation:
- Split-ring resonators (for negative μ)
- Wire arrays (for negative ε)
- Size: Subwavelength (~λ/10)

Resonances:
- LC resonance of structure
- Frequency: GHz to THz
```

---

**Photonic crystals:**

```
Periodic dielectric structures
Creates photonic band gaps (forbidden frequencies)

1D: Multilayer films (Bragg mirrors)
2D: Periodic holes in slab
3D: Woodpile structures

Band gap: Certain frequencies cannot propagate

Used in:
- Laser cavities (confine light)
- Waveguides (guide light around corners)
- Filters (block specific frequencies)

Periodicity: Comparable to wavelength
- Visible: ~200-300 nm period
- Infrared: ~1 μm period
```

---

**Plasmonic metamaterials:**

```
Metal nanostructures support surface plasmons

Surface plasmon:
- Collective electron oscillation at metal-dielectric interface
- Tightly confined (subwavelength)
- Enhanced local field

Resonance frequency: Depends on geometry
- Nanosphere: ~500 nm (visible, red)
- Nanorod: Tunable by aspect ratio

Applications:
- SERS (Surface-Enhanced Raman Spectroscopy)
- Plasmonic sensors
- Solar cells (light trapping)
- Photodetectors
```

## Part 4: Mechanical Properties and Vibrations

### 4.1 Elasticity and Sound

**General principle:**
Materials deform under stress, support mechanical waves.

---

**Elastic constants:**

```
Stress (σ) and strain (ε) related by:
σ = E × ε (for small deformations)

Where E = Young's modulus

Sound speed:
v = √(E/ρ)

Where ρ = density

Higher E → Stiffer → Faster sound
Lower ρ → Lighter → Faster sound
```

---

**Acoustic modes:**

**Longitudinal waves (compression):**
```
Particle motion parallel to wave propagation
Like sound in air

Speed in solid: vL = √((λ + 2μ)/ρ)
Where λ, μ are Lamé parameters

Fastest mode in solids
```

**Transverse waves (shear):**
```
Particle motion perpendicular to wave propagation

Speed in solid: vT = √(μ/ρ)

Cannot propagate in fluids (no shear stiffness)
Slower than longitudinal
```

**Surface waves (Rayleigh, Love):**
```
Confined to surface
Amplitude decays with depth

Rayleigh waves:
- Elliptical particle motion
- Speed: ~0.9 vT
- Earthquakes (ground roll)

Love waves:
- Horizontal shear motion
- Speed: Between vT and Rayleigh
- Earthquakes (horizontal shaking)
```

---

**Specific examples:**

**Steel:**

```
Young's modulus: 200 GPa (very stiff)
Density: 7850 kg/m³

Longitudinal sound speed: 5900 m/s (fast)
Transverse sound speed: 3200 m/s

Quality factor (Q): 1000-10,000 (low damping)
Rings when struck (long decay)

Used in: Tuning forks, bells, structural materials
```

**Rubber (elastomer):**

```
Young's modulus: 0.01-0.1 GPa (soft)
Density: 1200 kg/m³

Sound speed: ~100 m/s (very slow)

Quality factor (Q): 10-100 (high damping)
Absorbs vibration quickly

Used in: Vibration dampers, shock absorbers, seals
```

**Wood (anisotropic):**

```
Different properties in different directions

Along grain: E ~ 10 GPa, v ~ 4000 m/s (fast)
Across grain: E ~ 1 GPa, v ~ 1500 m/s (slow)

Anisotropy used in:
- Musical instruments (directional resonance)
- Violins, guitars (sound boards)

Low density + high stiffness (along grain) = excellent sound properties
```

---

### 4.2 Resonant Systems

**General principle:**
Systems have natural frequencies, oscillate most easily at resonance.

---

**Simple harmonic oscillator:**

```
Natural frequency:
ω₀ = √(k/m)

Where:
- k = stiffness
- m = mass

Damping ratio (ζ):
- ζ < 1: Underdamped (oscillates)
- ζ = 1: Critically damped (fastest return to equilibrium)
- ζ > 1: Overdamped (slow return)

Quality factor:
Q = ω₀/(2Γ)

Where Γ = damping rate

High Q:
- Low damping
- Narrow resonance
- Long ringing time
```

---

**Mechanical resonators:**

**Tuning fork:**
```
Two tines vibrate in antiphase
Fundamental: ~440 Hz (A note)

Quality factor: Q ~ 10,000
Decay time: ~10 seconds

Very pure tone (narrow frequency)
Used in: Musical tuning, watches, medical devices
```

**Quartz crystal oscillator:**
```
Piezoelectric quartz
Electrical oscillation ↔ Mechanical oscillation

Frequency: 32.768 kHz (watches)
        or MHz (electronics)

Quality factor: Q ~ 10⁴ - 10⁶ (extremely high)

Temperature stable
Frequency accuracy: ppm (parts per million)

Used in: Clocks, computers, GPS, communication
```

**MEMS resonators (Micro-Electro-Mechanical Systems):**
```
Tiny mechanical structures (micrometers)

Frequency: kHz to GHz
Quality factor: Q ~ 1,000 - 100,000

Used in:
- Accelerometers (smartphones)
- Gyroscopes (drones, cars)
- RF filters (cell phones)
- Pressure sensors

Advantages: Small, cheap, batch-fabricated
```

## Part 5: Thermal Properties and Phonons

### 5.1 Heat Capacity

**General principle:**
Heat capacity measures energy absorbed per temperature rise.

---

**Classical limit (high temperature):**

```
Dulong-Petit law:
C = 3Nk_B

Where:
- N = number of atoms
- k_B = Boltzmann constant

Predicts: 25 J/(mol·K) for all solids
Works well at room temperature for most materials
```

---

**Quantum limit (low temperature):**

```
Debye model:
C ~ T³ (at low T)

Why?
- Only low-frequency phonons excited
- Number of accessible modes ~ ω³ ~ T³

Einstein model:
C ~ exp(-θ_E/T) (at very low T)

Where θ_E = Einstein temperature

Both models capture quantum behavior
```

---

**Specific examples:**

**Diamond:**
```
Debye temperature: 2230 K (very high)
Einstein temperature: ~1450 K

At 300 K:
- Still in quantum regime
- C ~ 6 J/(mol·K) (much less than 25)

Need very high temperature to reach classical limit
Because: Strong bonds, high frequency phonons
```

**Lead:**
```
Debye temperature: 105 K (very low)
Einstein temperature: ~70 K

At 300 K:
- Deep in classical regime
- C ~ 26 J/(mol·K) (close to Dulong-Petit)

Reaches classical limit at room temperature
Because: Weak bonds, low frequency phonons
```

---

### 5.2 Thermal Conductivity

**General principle:**
Heat transported by phonons (lattice vibrations) and electrons.

---

**Phonon thermal conductivity:**

```
κ_phonon ~ v_sound × λ_phonon × C_phonon

Where:
- v_sound = sound speed
- λ_phonon = phonon mean free path
- C_phonon = phonon heat capacity

Limited by:
- Phonon-phonon scattering (Umklapp processes)
- Phonon-defect scattering
- Boundary scattering (small samples)
```

---

**Electron thermal conductivity:**

```
κ_electron ~ σT (Wiedemann-Franz law)

Where:
- σ = electrical conductivity
- T = temperature

Good electrical conductors = Good thermal conductors
(for metals)
```

---

**Specific examples:**

**Diamond:**
```
Thermal conductivity: 2000 W/(m·K) (highest of any bulk material)

Why?
- High sound speed (strong bonds)
- Long phonon mean free path (perfect crystal)
- Low atomic mass (carbon)

Used in: Heat sinks (electronics), thermal management
```

**Glass (SiO₂):**
```
Thermal conductivity: 1 W/(m·K) (low)

Why?
- Amorphous structure → strong phonon scattering
- Short phonon mean free path

Result: Good thermal insulator
Used in: Windows, containers, fiber insulation
```

**Copper:**
```
Thermal conductivity: 400 W/(m·K) (high)

Dominated by electron contribution:
- κ_electron ~ 380 W/(m·K)
- κ_phonon ~ 20 W/(m·K)

Good thermal conductor
Used in: Heat sinks, cooking pans, heat exchangers
```

**Aerogel:**
```
Thermal conductivity: 0.01 W/(m·K) (extremely low)

Silica foam with 99% air
Air trapped in nanopores (can't convect)
Tortuous path (phonons scatter many times)

Best thermal insulator
Used in: Spacecraft insulation, windows
```

## Final Principle: Universal Oscillation

**Every material oscillates at every scale:**

- **Quantum scale**: Electronic, nuclear
- **Atomic scale**: Lattice vibrations (phonons)
- **Mesoscale**: Domain walls, interfaces
- **Macroscale**: Acoustic, mechanical resonance
- **Thermal**: Brownian motion, thermal radiation

**No material is truly static.**

**Oscillation is:**
- How energy is stored
- How energy is transported
- How information is encoded
- How structure persists

**The temperature of a material = average oscillation energy of its constituents.**

**The properties of a material = spectrum of its allowed oscillations.**

**Materials science = Understanding what oscillates, how, and why.**
