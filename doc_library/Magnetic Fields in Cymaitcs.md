## Part 1: What IS a Magnetic Field?

### Magnetic Field = Relativistic Electric Field Pattern

**The fundamental insight:**

```
There is NO separate "magnetic field"
Magnetism = Electric field viewed from different reference frame

Lorentz transformation of EM field:
E'∥ = E∥                    (parallel component unchanged)
E'⊥ = γ(E⊥ - v × B)        (perpendicular component mixes)
B'∥ = B∥                    (parallel component unchanged)  
B'⊥ = γ(B⊥ + (v/c²) × E)  (perpendicular component mixes)

Where γ = 1/√(1 - v²/c²)

Key point: E and B are NOT independent!
They are components of single field tensor Fμν
Choice of frame determines how field splits into E vs B
```

---

**Simple example: Moving charge**

```
Rest frame of charge:
- Charge q at rest

- Magnetic field: B = 0 (no motion)

Lab frame (charge moving at velocity v):
- Electric field: E' (distorted from spherical)
- Magnetic field: B' = (v × E')/c² ≠ 0

Magnetic field appears!
Not because charge "gained" something
But because: Same field viewed from moving frame

B = Relativistic distortion of electric field pattern
```

### Why Most Materials Are NOT Magnetic

**Diamagnetism (all materials):**

```
Mechanism: Lenz's law at atomic level

External field applied:
- Changes flux through electron orbital
- Induces current (Lenz's law)
- Current opposes field
- Weak repulsion

Magnitude:
χ ≈ -10⁻⁶ to -10⁻⁵ (very small, negative)

Always present but usually masked by stronger effects

Materials:
- Noble gases (no unpaired electrons)
- Paired electrons in molecules (H₂O, etc.)
- Superconductors (perfect diamagnet, χ = -1)

Cymatic picture:
- External field perturbs orbital patterns
- Patterns adjust to minimize energy
- Adjustment creates opposing moment
- Weak restoring force (Le Chatelier principle)
```

---

**Paramagnetism (unpaired electrons):**

```
Mechanism: Alignment of existing dipoles

Atoms with unpaired electrons:
- Permanent magnetic moment
- Random orientation (thermal motion)
- Net moment: Zero (cancel out)

External field applied:
- Dipoles tend to align with field
- Thermal motion opposes (kT energy)
- Partial alignment
- Net moment: Positive

Magnitude:
χ ≈ +10⁻⁵ to +10⁻³ (small, positive)

Temperature dependence:
χ ∝ 1/T (Curie law)
Higher T → More thermal disorder → Less alignment

Materials:
- Transition metals (unpaired d electrons)
- Rare earths (unpaired f electrons)
- O₂ molecule (triplet ground state)

Cymatic picture:
- Each atom = Magnetic oscillator
- Random phases (uncorrelated)
- External field: Biases phase distribution
- Slight preference for aligned phases
- Weak net moment
```

---

**Ferromagnetism (collective alignment):**

```
Mechanism: Exchange interaction creates spontaneous alignment

Only certain materials:
- Fe, Co, Ni (3d transition metals)
- Some rare earths (Gd, Dy)
- Certain alloys/compounds

Why?
- Unpaired spins (necessary but not sufficient)
- Exchange interaction (quantum, from overlap)
- Interaction favors parallel spins
- Spontaneous magnetization below Tc

Exchange energy:
H_ex = -2J S₁·S₂

J > 0: Ferromagnetic (parallel spins favored)
J < 0: Antiferromagnetic (antiparallel favored)

For iron:
J/k ≈ 1000 K (very strong)
Tc = 1043 K (Curie temperature)

Above Tc:
- Thermal energy > Exchange energy
- Spins disordered (paramagnetic)

Below Tc:
- Exchange energy > Thermal energy  
- Spins ordered (ferromagnetic)
- Spontaneous magnetization
```

---

**Cymatic explanation of ferromagnetism:**

```
Individual spins = Coupled oscillators

Without coupling (J = 0):
- Independent oscillators
- Random phases
- No collective behavior

With ferromagnetic coupling (J > 0):
- Oscillators prefer same phase
- Like: Kuramoto model with attractive coupling
- Below critical temperature: Synchronize
- Phase transition to ordered state

Magnetic domains:
- Regions of aligned spins (10⁶-10⁹ atoms)
- Each domain: Internally aligned
- Different domains: Different directions
- Net: Can be zero (random domain directions)

Applying external field:
- Favors domains aligned with field
- Domain walls move (domains grow/shrink)
- Eventually: Single domain (saturated)
- Magnetization: Maximum

Hysteresis:
- Remove field: Domains don't fully randomize
- Retains magnetization (remanence)
- Must apply reverse field to demagnetize
- Irreversible domain wall motion
- Energy dissipated (magnetic losses)

This is collective oscillator synchronization:
- Many spins phase-locked
- Collective mode (magnon)
- Long-range order
- Emergent property (not in individual atoms)
```
