- Resists the distortion (pushes back)
- Begins reconstructing with the new asymmetry encoded (starts moving)

Force applied over time = momentum accumulated.

Resistance to having your reconstruction bias altered.

## Friction

**Friction is pattern interference at boundaries.**

---

Two surfaces in contact. Each surface is a lattice—a resonance geometry with a specific structure.

They don't match. Different frequencies. Different node spacings. Different phases.

When you try to slide one past the other, the patterns *catch.* Nodes interlock momentarily. Small-scale constructive interference creates temporary bonds.

To move, you must *break* those bonds. Costs energy. That energy becomes jitter (heat).

---

**Static friction:**

The patterns are at rest relative to each other. They've settled into local interference minima. Nodes have found temporary locks.

To start moving, you must overcome *all* the current interlocks simultaneously.

High threshold. Patterns are dug in.

---

**Kinetic friction:**

Already moving. Patterns can't fully settle. Interlocks form and break continuously.

Lower threshold than static. The patterns never fully lock—they're skipping across each other's geometry.

But still losing coherence at every catch-and-release. Still generating noise.

---

**Why roughness matters:**

Rough surface = more complex interference geometry. More places for patterns to catch.

Smooth surface = simpler geometry. Fewer catch points.

Polish something smooth → reduce the interference complexity → reduce friction.

---

**Why lubricants work:**

Interpose a third pattern between the two surfaces. One that doesn't lock strongly with either.

The lubricant's geometry slides easily against both surfaces. It's *frequency-compatible* with sliding.

Oil molecules: long chains that align with motion. They shear past each other readily. Low catch.

---

**Friction at the limit:**

Two perfectly smooth, perfectly clean surfaces of the same material?

They *cold weld.* Patterns so compatible they lock completely. Become one piece.

Friction isn't just resistance. It's *incomplete bonding.* Failed merger. Surfaces that almost become one, but keep getting torn apart.

---

**Heat from friction:**

Every broken interlock releases energy. But it's not coherent—it sprays in random directions.

Jitter. Noise. Heat.

Friction converts organized motion into disorganized motion. Coherence into incoherence.

This is why it's "lossy." You can't get the organization back without doing work.

## Surface Tension

**Surface tension is boundary coherence cost.**

---

Inside a liquid: patterns surrounded by similar patterns on all sides. Resonance needs met equally in every direction. Stable.

At the surface: patterns have neighbors below and beside, but *nothing above.* Asymmetric environment.

The surface patterns must maintain coherence with only partial support. They pull inward, toward the bulk where resonance needs are fully met.

---

**The "tension":**

It's not a membrane. It's not a skin.

It's the *energy cost of being at a boundary.* Surface patterns are in a strained state. They'd rather be inside.

The liquid minimizes surface area because surface area is *coherence debt.*

---

**Why spheres:**

Sphere has minimum surface area for a given volume.

Droplets form spheres because that's the geometry that minimizes the number of patterns stuck in the strained boundary state.

Not "trying" to be spheres. Just settling into lowest energy configuration.

---

**Why water has high surface tension:**

Hydrogen bonding. Water patterns form strong, directional resonance locks with each other.

At the surface, those locks are *missing* on one side. The asymmetry is severe. The pull inward is strong.

---

**Why surfactants reduce surface tension:**

Soap molecules have two ends: one water-compatible (hydrophilic), one not (hydrophobic).

They migrate to the surface. Hydrophilic end locks with water patterns. Hydrophobic end faces the air.

Now the surface patterns have *something* to resonate with on the air side. The asymmetry is reduced. Less strain. Lower tension.

---

**Capillary action:**

Water in a thin tube. The water patterns resonate well with the tube wall (adhesion). Sometimes better than they resonate with each other (cohesion).

The water climbs the walls to maximize pattern-wall contact. Pulls the surface into a curve (meniscus). In narrow tubes, can climb significantly.

Adhesion > cohesion → patterns prefer wall contact → liquid rises.

Cohesion > adhesion → patterns prefer each other → liquid depresses (like mercury).

---

**Insects walking on water:**

Their feet don't break through because their weight is distributed enough that the *coherence cost* of breaking the surface exceeds the *compression* they impose.

The surface bends but doesn't break. The boundary patterns strain but maintain.

Once the strain exceeds the coherence threshold → they fall through.

---

**Bubbles:**

Thin film of liquid. Two surfaces. Patterns strained on both sides.

Minimizes area → spherical.

The air inside is at higher pressure—it has to be, to balance the inward pull of both surfaces.

Pop a bubble: you break the coherence. The strained patterns release. Surface energy converts to motion and jitter.

Both friction and surface tension are about **boundaries.**

Friction: boundary between *different* pattern systems. Interference and catch.

Surface tension: boundary between pattern system and *nothing* (or non-resonant medium). Coherence strain.

Both are *energetically expensive.* Systems minimize both when possible.

Both convert organized structure into heat when boundaries are disrupted.

---

**The deeper principle:**

Patterns want complete resonance environments. Boundaries are where that completeness fails.

Friction is *failed merger.*
Surface tension is *incomplete belonging.*

Both are the cost of being at the edge.
